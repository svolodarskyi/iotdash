# IoTDash — Why They Buy: Understanding Business Pain Before You Sell

> **Purpose:** What to be curious about when you talk to any business. How to discover their real pain, how to recognize buying signals, and how different industries think about monitoring.

---

## 1. Nobody Buys Monitoring. They Buy Relief From Fear.

Every purchase decision is driven by one of four emotions:

```
FEAR:    "What if something goes wrong and I don't know?"
PAIN:    "I'm already wasting time/money on this problem."
GREED:   "I could make more money if I had this."
MANDATE: "Someone is forcing me to do this (regulator, insurer, boss)."
```

**Your product hits all four:**
- Fear: freezer fails at 3 AM, nobody knows
- Pain: walking around with a clipboard takes 2 hours a day
- Greed: if I optimized my energy usage, I'd save $500/month
- Mandate: HACCP audit is in 6 weeks and I need continuous logs

**The strongest motivator is MANDATE + FEAR.** "I have to do this, and if I don't, something bad happens." That's why regulated industries (food, pharma) buy fastest.

## 2. The Questions to Ask Every Business

You're not selling when you have these conversations. You're **learning**. The goal is to understand their world so well that when you eventually pitch, it sounds like you read their mind.

### 2.1 The Discovery Framework

Every business you talk to, you want to understand 6 things:

```
1. WHAT DO THEY MONITOR?       (or should monitor but don't)
2. HOW DO THEY MONITOR NOW?    (manual, existing system, not at all)
3. WHAT GOES WRONG?            (the horror stories)
4. WHAT DOES IT COST?          (when things go wrong)
5. WHO CARES?                  (who feels the pain, who pays for solutions)
6. WHAT FORCES THEM TO ACT?    (regulation, insurance, a recent disaster)
```

### 2.2 The Actual Questions (Conversation, Not Interrogation)

**Opening (2 minutes):**
```
"I'm building a remote monitoring system for [industry].
I'm trying to understand how companies like yours handle
[temperature/equipment/environment] monitoring.

I'm not here to sell anything — I'm genuinely trying to learn
how your world works. Can I ask you a few questions?"
```

**About their current situation:**
```
"Walk me through your day — when does monitoring come into play?"
"How do you currently keep track of [temperatures/equipment/conditions]?"
"Who's responsible for checking? How often?"
"What happens on weekends and holidays? Night shifts?"
"Do you have any automated monitoring, or is it all manual?"
"What does your current process look like?" (ask them to SHOW you if on-site)
```

**About what goes wrong:**
```
"When was the last time something went wrong with [equipment/storage]?"
"What happened? How did you find out?"
"How long before someone noticed?"
"What did it cost you?" (product loss, repair, downtime, fine)
"Has that happened more than once?"
"What's the WORST thing that could happen? The nightmare scenario?"
```

**About the cost of problems:**
```
"Roughly, what's in [that freezer/on that production line] at any time?"
"If you lost all of it, what would that cost?"
"How much does unplanned downtime cost per hour?"
"Have you ever failed an audit? What happened?"
"What does your insurance say about monitoring?"
```

**About what forces change:**
```
"Do you have regulatory requirements for monitoring?"
"Has your insurer ever asked about this?"
"Has your boss/management asked you to improve this?"
"Have any of your clients asked for monitoring records?"
"Are your competitors doing this already?"
```

**About buying:**
```
"If you could wave a magic wand, what would monitoring look like for you?"
"Have you looked at solutions before? What happened?"
"What would make you pull the trigger on something like this?"
"What would prevent you from doing it?"
"Who else would need to approve this kind of purchase?"
```

**Closing:**
```
"This is really helpful. I'm building something that might address
some of what you described. When I have it ready, can I show you?"
```

## 3. Industry-by-Industry: What to Be Curious About

### 3.1 Cold Storage / Food Distribution

```
WHAT THEY MONITOR:
  - Freezer temperatures (-18C to -22C typically)
  - Cooler temperatures (0C to 4C)
  - Loading dock temperatures during receiving
  - Transport vehicle temperatures (sometimes)

WHAT TO ASK:
  "How many cold rooms / freezers / coolers do you have?"
  "How often do you do temperature checks?"
  "Who does the checks? What if they're sick?"
  "Do you use paper logs or digital?"
  "When was your last HACCP / food safety audit?"
  "How much prep time does an audit take?"
  "Have you ever had a compressor fail overnight?"
  "What's the most expensive product loss you've had?"
  "What's stored in your largest freezer right now, in dollars?"

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR:
  "We check every 4 hours. It's a pain."
  "Someone forgot to log on Saturday and we had to explain to the auditor."
  "We had a compressor fail on a long weekend. Lost $30K of product."
  "Our insurance requires monitoring but we do it manually."
  "Audit prep takes 2-3 days of organizing paper logs."

BUYING TRIGGER:
  → Upcoming audit (urgency)
  → Recent product loss (pain is fresh)
  → Insurance requirement (mandate)
  → New food safety regulations (mandate)
  → Expansion (new facility = fresh start, easier to adopt new systems)

PRICE SENSITIVITY:
  Medium. They're used to paying for equipment. $29/device is nothing
  compared to what's in the freezer. ROI argument is easy.

DECISION MAKER:    Operations Manager or Quality/Food Safety Manager
BLOCKER:           Owner who thinks manual checks are "good enough"
BUDGET:            $200-500/month is easy to approve
SALES CYCLE:       2-4 weeks (short — they feel the pain daily)
```

### 3.2 Restaurants / Commercial Kitchens

```
WHAT THEY MONITOR:
  - Walk-in cooler and freezer temperatures
  - Prep fridge temperatures
  - Dishwasher water temperature (health code)
  - Sometimes: hood exhaust, grease trap

WHAT TO ASK:
  "How do you handle HACCP checks during a busy service?"
  "Who's responsible for temp logs when the kitchen is slammed?"
  "Has a health inspector ever flagged your temp records?"
  "How many locations do you have?" (chains = volume opportunity)
  "What would happen if your walk-in died overnight?"

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR:
  "We're supposed to check every 2 hours but honestly it doesn't always happen."
  "The health inspector wants to see continuous logs. We have paper."
  "Our walk-in compressor is old and I worry about it."
  "I manage 5 locations and I can't be everywhere."

BUYING TRIGGER:
  → Health inspection failure or warning
  → Multi-location management (can't be everywhere)
  → Insurance renewal asking about monitoring
  → Recent equipment failure

PRICE SENSITIVITY:
  High. Restaurants run on thin margins. Need to be cheap.
  $15-20/device, or a flat rate per location ($49/month for 3 sensors).

DECISION MAKER:    Owner or General Manager
SALES CYCLE:       1-2 weeks (fast decision, small business)
```

### 3.3 Warehousing / Logistics

```
WHAT THEY MONITOR:
  - Ambient temperature and humidity (product integrity)
  - Cold storage zones
  - Dock door open/close (energy loss, security)
  - Sometimes: CO2 levels, pest traps

WHAT TO ASK:
  "What kinds of goods do you store? Any temperature-sensitive?"
  "Do your clients require proof of storage conditions?"
  "How do you handle multiple zones with different requirements?"
  "What happens when a dock door gets left open?"
  "Do you provide temperature records to clients?"
  "How many square feet / zones? How many buildings?"

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR:
  "Our pharmaceutical client requires continuous temp logs for their products."
  "We lost a client because we couldn't prove cold chain compliance."
  "In summer, the dock side of the warehouse heats up and nobody notices for hours."
  "We're growing and adding a new building — need monitoring there too."

BUYING TRIGGER:
  → Client requirement (their client demands monitoring records)
  → Client loss due to lack of monitoring proof
  → Facility expansion (new building = natural entry point)
  → Insurance or lease requirement

PRICE SENSITIVITY:
  Low-Medium. Warehousing is B2B, margins are decent.
  They'll pay $29-49/device if it helps them keep a client.

DECISION MAKER:    Facility Manager or Operations Director
SALES CYCLE:       3-6 weeks (medium — may need corporate approval)
```

### 3.4 Manufacturing (Small-Medium)

```
WHAT THEY MONITOR:
  - Machine temperature (motors, bearings, hydraulics)
  - Vibration (rotating equipment)
  - Power consumption (energy, abnormal draw = problem)
  - Compressed air pressure
  - Environmental (paint booth temp/humidity, cleanroom conditions)

WHAT TO ASK:
  "Which machine would hurt the most if it went down unexpectedly?"
  "How much does an hour of downtime cost you?"
  "How do you currently know if a machine has a problem?"
  "Do you do preventive maintenance? On what schedule?"
  "Have you ever had a machine fail with no warning?"
  "What does a typical repair cost? Emergency vs planned?"
  "How many shifts do you run? Who watches equipment overnight?"

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR:
  "Our CNC machine went down last month. 3 days to get parts. $40K lost."
  "The night shift just watches for obvious problems. Nobody monitors gauges."
  "We do maintenance every 3 months whether it needs it or not. Probably wasteful."
  "Our compressor room overheated last summer and nobody knew for 6 hours."

BUYING TRIGGER:
  → Recent expensive breakdown (pain is fresh)
  → Adding a night shift (nobody to watch equipment)
  → Insurance pushing for monitoring
  → New equipment purchase (protect the investment)
  → Lean/continuous improvement initiative

PRICE SENSITIVITY:
  Medium. They understand equipment costs. If you frame it as
  "insurance for your $200K machine at $29/month," it's trivial.

DECISION MAKER:    Plant Manager or Maintenance Manager
BLOCKER:           "We've always done it this way"
SALES CYCLE:       4-8 weeks (need to see it on THEIR machine to believe)
```

### 3.5 Commercial HVAC / Facility Management

```
WHAT THEY MONITOR:
  - Zone temperatures (tenant comfort)
  - HVAC unit status (running/off/fault)
  - Energy consumption
  - Humidity (mold prevention)
  - Water leak detection

WHAT TO ASK:
  "How many buildings / zones do you manage?"
  "How do you hear about temperature complaints?"
  "What's your energy bill? Has it been going up?"
  "Have you ever had a tenant leave over comfort issues?"
  "Do you have a BMS (Building Management System)? How old is it?"
  "How do you handle off-hours issues? Who gets called at 2 AM?"
  "Have you ever had a pipe burst or water leak?"
  "How much of your time is reactive vs proactive?"

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR:
  "I manage 8 buildings. I find out about problems when tenants complain."
  "Our BMS is 15 years old and the vendor wants $50K to upgrade."
  "Energy costs have gone up 30% and I can't figure out where it's going."
  "We had a pipe burst on a weekend. $200K in water damage."
  "I spend 80% of my time putting out fires. I never get ahead."

BUYING TRIGGER:
  → Tenant complaints / tenant loss
  → Energy cost spike
  → BMS end-of-life (yours is cheaper than a new BMS)
  → Water damage event (leak detection add-on)
  → Sustainability / ESG reporting requirements

PRICE SENSITIVITY:
  Low. Facility budgets are large. $29/device is rounding error.
  But decision-making can be slow (corporate property management).

DECISION MAKER:    Facility Manager or Property Manager
BLOCKER:           Corporate procurement process
SALES CYCLE:       4-12 weeks (can be slow, especially corporate real estate)
```

### 3.6 Pharmacy / Healthcare

```
WHAT THEY MONITOR:
  - Medication fridge temperature (2-8C)
  - Vaccine storage temperature
  - Blood bank temperature
  - Lab incubator temperature
  - Ambient conditions (some drugs are humidity-sensitive)

WHAT TO ASK:
  "How do you track fridge temperatures? Manual or automated?"
  "What's the total value of what's in your medication fridge?"
  "Have you ever had a fridge fail? What happened to the stock?"
  "What does GDP (Good Distribution Practice) require for your records?"
  "How does your current system alert you to out-of-range temperatures?"
  "Who is responsible for temperature checks on weekends?"
  "Have you had a regulatory inspection recently? How did it go?"

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR:
  "We check twice a day and log it on paper."
  "Our fridge has $80K of insulin in it at any time."
  "We had a fridge fail on a bank holiday. Nobody found out until Tuesday."
  "Our regulator wants continuous digital records. We're not there yet."
  "We have 12 branches. Each one manages their own fridge monitoring."

BUYING TRIGGER:
  → Regulatory requirement (GDP, GLP, GMP)
  → Recent stock loss from fridge failure
  → Multi-site management (pharmacy chains)
  → Upcoming inspection

PRICE SENSITIVITY:
  Very Low. Pharma has high margins. $29/device to protect $80K of drugs
  is a no-brainer. They'll also pay premium for validated/certified systems.

DECISION MAKER:    Pharmacy Manager, Quality Manager, or Compliance Officer
SALES CYCLE:       4-8 weeks (regulatory process, but strong urgency)
```

### 3.7 Agriculture / Greenhouses

```
WHAT THEY MONITOR:
  - Soil moisture
  - Air temperature and humidity
  - Light levels
  - CO2 concentration
  - Irrigation status (on/off, flow rate)
  - Weather station data

WHAT TO ASK:
  "What do you grow? How sensitive is it to temperature/moisture?"
  "How do you decide when to water?"
  "Have you ever lost a crop to frost, heat, or drought?"
  "How far are your fields/greenhouses from where you live?"
  "Do you check conditions at night?"
  "How much is one greenhouse worth in a growing season?"
  "What does crop insurance look like? Do they ask about monitoring?"

WHAT YOU'LL HEAR:
  "I drove 30 minutes to the greenhouse at midnight because I thought it was too cold."
  "We lost $15K of seedlings to one frost night."
  "I water on a schedule, but I know it's not optimal."
  "My neighbor has some sensor thing. I've been thinking about it."

BUYING TRIGGER:
  → Crop loss event
  → Expansion (new greenhouse = new investment to protect)
  → Water cost / conservation requirements
  → Neighbor/competitor using monitoring (peer pressure)

PRICE SENSITIVITY:
  High. Agriculture runs on thin margins. Need affordable entry.
  $15-25/device, or bundled per greenhouse ($49-99/month).

DECISION MAKER:    Owner (usually a sole proprietor or family business)
SALES CYCLE:       1-3 weeks (owner decides fast, no bureaucracy)
```

## 4. The Universal Buying Signals

During any conversation, listen for these phrases. They tell you where the prospect is in their decision process.

### 4.1 "Not Ready" Signals (Nurture, Don't Push)

```
"We're fine with how things are."
  → No pain. Not a prospect right now. Move on.

"We looked at something like this a few years ago."
  → Mildly interested. Ask: "What stopped you then?"

"That sounds interesting."
  → Polite. Doesn't mean anything. Keep qualifying.

"Let me think about it."
  → Not convinced. Ask: "What would help you decide?"
```

### 4.2 "Getting Warm" Signals (Push Gently)

```
"How much does it cost?"
  → They're mentally buying. Answer directly, then offer the pilot.

"Can it do [specific thing]?"
  → They're mapping it to their situation. Good sign.

"We had [bad thing] happen recently."
  → Pain is fresh. This is your opening.

"My boss has been asking about this."
  → External pressure. They need a solution to bring to their boss.

"Our auditor mentioned continuous monitoring."
  → Regulatory trigger. Strong.

"How long does installation take?"
  → They're imagining it in their facility. Very good.
```

### 4.3 "Ready to Buy" Signals (Close Now)

```
"Can you come take a look at our facility?"
  → They want to see if it fits. Bring a sensor.

"What would it look like for our 12 freezers?"
  → They're scoping the deal. Give them a quote.

"Can we do a trial?"
  → YES. Set up the pilot immediately.

"Who else in our industry uses this?"
  → They want social proof before committing. Case study time.

"Can we start with just 2-3 to test?"
  → This IS the pilot. Say yes. Install this week.

"I need to show this to my manager."
  → They're sold. Give them materials to sell internally.
```

## 5. The Real Reasons People Buy (and Don't Buy)

### 5.1 Why They BUY

| Reason | Frequency | What They Say | What They Mean |
|--------|:---:|-------------|---------------|
| **Fear of loss** | Very common | "We can't afford another freezer failure." | "I'll get fired if this happens again." |
| **Regulatory compliance** | Common | "We need continuous records for our audit." | "The regulator will shut us down without this." |
| **Time savings** | Common | "Manual checks take too long." | "I hate the clipboard walk and I want my weekends back." |
| **Peace of mind** | Common | "I just want to know everything's OK." | "I check my phone at midnight worrying about the freezer." |
| **Boss told them to** | Common | "Management wants us to modernize." | "I need to bring them something or I look incompetent." |
| **Insurance** | Occasional | "Our insurer mentioned monitoring." | "Our premium might go down, or they might not renew without it." |
| **Competitive pressure** | Occasional | "Our competitors have this." | "If we don't catch up, we lose clients." |
| **Client requirement** | Occasional | "Our biggest client is asking for temp records." | "If we lose this client, we're in trouble." |

### 5.2 Why They DON'T Buy

| Reason | What They Say | What They Mean | How to Handle |
|--------|-------------|---------------|--------------|
| **No perceived pain** | "We've never had a problem." | "I don't believe bad things will happen to me." | Share industry statistics. "Your competitor thought the same thing before their $50K loss." |
| **Cost** | "It's too expensive." | "I don't believe the ROI." | ROI calculator. "How much is in your freezer right now? Our annual cost is 0.5% of what you're protecting." |
| **Status quo bias** | "Our current process works fine." | "Change is scary and I'm busy." | Don't fight this. Offer the free pilot. "You don't have to change. Just let us run alongside for 2 weeks." |
| **Bad past experience** | "We tried something like this before." | "It was unreliable/complicated/expensive." | "What went wrong?" Then explain how yours is different. Usually: simpler. |
| **Decision paralysis** | "I need to think about it." | "I'm not the decision maker" or "I don't have enough info." | "What would help you decide? Would it help if I talked to [boss/IT]?" |
| **IT resistance** | "Our IT department won't approve this." | "IT doesn't want another system to manage." | "It's fully hosted. Your IT doesn't need to do anything. It's just WiFi." |
| **Timing** | "Not right now." | Could be real, could be a brush-off. | "When would be a better time? Can I check back in [month]?" Put in CRM, follow up. |

## 6. How to Map Pain to Your Product

### 6.1 The Pain → Feature → Outcome Table

When you hear a pain, connect it to what you've built:

| Pain You Hear | Feature You Show | Outcome You Promise |
|--------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| "Manual checks take too long" | Live dashboard on phone | "Check all devices in 10 seconds from anywhere" |
| "We found the problem too late" | Email alerts | "You'll know within 60 seconds, day or night" |
| "Audit prep takes days" | PDF/CSV export | "Generate your audit report in 30 seconds" |
| "Nobody checks on weekends" | 24/7 automated monitoring | "The system checks every 30 seconds. It never takes a day off." |
| "I can't tell if the sensor is working" | Online/offline status | "Green dot = working. Red dot = investigate." |
| "We lost product because nobody noticed" | Alert + escalation | "If you don't respond in 30 min, it alerts your manager" |
| "I manage multiple locations" | Multi-site dashboard | "All locations on one screen. See everything at a glance." |
| "Our readings are all over the place" | Trend chart + averages | "See the real trend, not the noise. Spot problems developing." |
| "My boss wants a weekly report" | Weekly email digest | "Automatic summary every Monday. No work required." |
| "What if your system goes down?" | Uptime tracking + redundancy | "99.9% uptime. If a sensor loses connection, you get alerted." |

### 6.2 Features You DON'T Need to Mention

| Feature | Why Not to Lead With It |
|---------|----------------------|
| MQTT protocol | Nobody cares about the protocol. They care about reliability. |
| InfluxDB / TimescaleDB | They don't know what a time-series database is. |
| Grafana | They don't know what Grafana is. It's "your dashboard." |
| API / Webhooks | Only mention if THEY ask about integration. |
| Edge computing | Save for technical buyers (IT managers). |
| Machine learning / AI | Don't mention unless you can prove it works. Buzzwords without proof = distrust. |
| Microservices architecture | Literally nobody outside engineering cares. |
| Multi-tenant isolation | They don't know or care. "Your data is private and secure." |

## 8. Building Your Curiosity Muscle

### 8.1 What "Good Curiosity" Looks Like in Practice

```
BAD:  "Do you need temperature monitoring?"  (closed question, forces yes/no)
GOOD: "How do you keep track of temperatures in your facility?" (open, invites story)

BAD:  "We can alert you when temperatures go out of range."  (pitching, not learning)
GOOD: "What happens when a temperature goes out of range? How do you find out?" (learning)

BAD:  "Our system checks every 30 seconds."  (feature dump)
GOOD: "How often do you check right now? What happens between checks?" (understanding gap)

BAD:  "You should switch to automated monitoring."  (telling, not selling)
GOOD: "What would change for you if you didn't have to do manual checks?" (letting them sell themselves)
```

### 8.3 Practice Opportunities (Without Prospects)

You don't have to wait for real prospects to practice:

| Practice With | What You Learn |
|--------------|---------------|
| **Your local supermarket** | Walk the frozen aisle. Look at the thermometers. Ask the manager how they monitor. |
| **Your office building** | Ask the facility manager how they track HVAC. What's their BMS? Do they get complaints? |
| **A restaurant you eat at** | Ask the owner about kitchen temp checks. How does the health inspector visit go? |
| **Your gym** | They have a pool, sauna, HVAC. How do they monitor? |
| **A farmer at a market** | Ask about frost protection, irrigation timing. How do they know when to act? |
| **Your partner's current/past clients** | He's worked on hardware in the field. What did THOSE clients complain about? |

**Every business monitors something. Start noticing.**

## 9. The One Thing to Remember

```
You are not selling a monitoring platform.
You are selling the answer to: "What happens at 3 AM on a Saturday?"

Every business owner lies awake worrying about SOMETHING.
Your job is to find out what that something is.
If it involves temperature, equipment, or conditions — you can help.
If it doesn't — you can't. Move on.

The conversation is not about YOUR product.
It's about THEIR problem.

Find the problem. Then show how you solve it.
That's the entire sale.
```
