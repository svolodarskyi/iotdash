# IoTDash — Time Management, AI Delegation, and Building With a Family

> **Purpose:** How to organize your time realistically. What to delegate to AI. How to keep the startup moving when you have a day job, a 5-year-old, and your partner is about to have a newborn.

## 2. How to Organize Your Time

### 2.1 The Weekly Template

**Your week (platform-focused):**

```
MONDAY-FRIDAY (Day job days)
───────────────────────────
  6:00-6:15    Wake up
  6:15-7:15    ★ STARTUP HOUR (before kid wakes up)
               Best brain time. Platform coding.
               No email. No Slack. No scrolling. Code only.
  7:15-8:00    Kid routine (breakfast, school prep)
  8:00-17:00   Day job
  17:00-19:00  Kid time (park, dinner, bedtime routine)
  19:00-19:30  Household
  19:30-20:00  Transition (shower, coffee/tea, decompress)
  20:00-21:30  ★ STARTUP BLOCK (3 weeknights, not all 5)
               Platform coding, AI delegation, or networking
  21:30-22:30  Personal time / partner time
  22:30        Sleep

  STARTUP TIME ON WORKDAYS: 1h morning + 1.5h evening (3 nights) = 9.5 hrs

SATURDAY
────────
  7:00-8:00    Family breakfast
  8:00-12:00   ★ STARTUP BLOCK (big block — this is where progress happens)
               Demo prep, platform coding, partner sync, pilot work
  12:00-18:00  Family time (full afternoon — non-negotiable)
  18:00-20:00  Family dinner, kid bedtime
  20:00-21:30  ★ STARTUP BLOCK (optional — only if energy permits)

  STARTUP TIME ON SATURDAY: 4-5.5 hrs

SUNDAY
──────
  Family day. REST.
  Exception: 30 min LinkedIn post + 30 min admin/email max.

  STARTUP TIME ON SUNDAY: 0-1 hr

WEEKLY TOTAL: 10-15 hours
```

### 2.2 The Three Rules

```
RULE 1: MORNING HOUR IS SACRED
  The 6:15-7:15 slot is your highest-quality hour.
  No meetings, no notifications, no "just checking email."
  Code, think, or create. Nothing else.
  This one hour, 5 days a week = 5 hours of DEEP work.
  Worth more than 15 hours of tired evening work.

RULE 2: NOT EVERY EVENING
  3 weeknights max. You MUST have 2 evenings for yourself
  and your partner. Burnout kills startups faster than
  competition.

  Pick your 3 nights. Stick to them. Tell your partner
  which nights are startup nights so they can plan.

RULE 3: SATURDAY AFTERNOON AND SUNDAY ARE FAMILY
  Your 5-year-old doesn't care about your startup.
  They care that you're at the park.
  Protect this fiercely. You will never get these years back.
  The startup can wait. The childhood can't.
```

### 2.3 Your Partner's Schedule (Post-Baby Reality)

```
MONTHS 1-3 (Survival Mode):
  Partner does: almost nothing for startup. That's OK.
  What he CAN do:
    - Answer questions async (text/voice memo)
    - Review hardware decisions (10 min here and there)
    - Think about hardware design while feeding the baby at 3 AM
       (seriously — this is when some good ideas happen)
  Target: 2-5 hours/week, flexible, zero scheduled commitments

MONTHS 3-6 (Recovery):
  Partner does: hardware work in short sessions
  What he CAN do:
    - 1-2 hour blocks on evenings/weekends
    - Sensor testing, firmware updates
    - One client call/week if needed
  Target: 5-8 hours/week

MONTHS 6+ (New Normal):
  Partner does: regular contribution
  Back to: 8-12 hours/week with a sustainable routine
```

**Critical: Don't guilt-trip your partner about low contribution during the baby months. If you do, you'll lose the partnership.** Cover for him now. He'll cover for you later when life hits you.

## 3. What to Delegate to AI (Claude, ChatGPT, etc.)

### 3.1 The Delegation Principle

```
DO YOURSELF:     Decisions, client relationships, core code architecture
DELEGATE TO AI:  First drafts, research, boilerplate, repetitive work, content

AI is your unpaid intern who works at 3 AM and never complains.
Use it that way.
```

### 3.2 Things AI Can Do for You RIGHT NOW

#### Writing & Content (Save 3-5 hrs/week)

| Task | How to Delegate | Time Saved |
|------|----------------|:---:|
| **LinkedIn posts** | "Write a LinkedIn post about [what I learned this week about cold storage monitoring]. Tone: casual, honest, not salesy. 150 words max." | 30 min/post |
| **Email outreach** | "Write a personalized cold email to an operations manager at a cold storage company. Mention [specific detail about their company]. Offer a free pilot." | 15 min/email |
| **Follow-up emails** | "Write a follow-up email to [person] I met at [event]. We talked about [topic]. I want to suggest a coffee meeting." | 10 min |
| **Blog posts / articles** | "Write a 500-word article: 'HACCP Temperature Monitoring Requirements in 2026'. Target audience: food storage operations managers. Plain English, no jargon." | 1-2 hrs/article |
| **Client summaries** | "Here's the raw data from our pilot. Write a 1-page summary for the client: devices monitored, uptime, alerts fired, key findings, recommendation." | 30 min |
| **Proposal / quote** | "Write a proposal for [company] to monitor 10 freezers at $29/device/month. Include: what they get, installation process, timeline, pricing breakdown." | 30 min |
| **Testimonial request** | "Write an email asking our client [name] for a testimonial. Make it easy for them — suggest a quote they can approve or edit." | 10 min |

#### Research (Save 2-3 hrs/week)

| Task | How to Delegate | Time Saved |
|------|----------------|:---:|
| **Prospect research** | "Research [company name]. What do they do? How many locations? Who's the operations manager? Any recent news about food safety or cold storage?" | 20 min/company |
| **Competitor analysis** | "Find 5 IoT temperature monitoring companies. What do they charge? What features do they emphasize? What do their reviews say?" | 1-2 hrs |
| **Regulatory research** | "Summarize the HACCP temperature monitoring requirements for cold storage in [country/state]. What's legally required?" | 1 hr |
| **Technology research** | "Compare ESP32-S3 vs ESP32-C3 for a battery-powered temperature sensor. Power consumption, WiFi range, price, availability." | 30 min |
| **Market sizing** | "How many cold storage facilities are there in [region]? What's the average number of cold rooms? What's the market size for temperature monitoring?" | 1 hr |
| **Event research** | "Find industry events and trade shows related to cold storage, food safety, or facility management in [region] in the next 6 months." | 30 min |

#### Code & Technical (Save 3-5 hrs/week)

| Task | How to Delegate | Time Saved |
|------|----------------|:---:|
| **Boilerplate code** | "Write a FastAPI CRUD router for a 'devices' resource with SQLAlchemy. Include create, read, list, update, delete." | 30-60 min |
| **Database migrations** | "Write an Alembic migration to add these columns to the devices table: [list]. Generate the upgrade and downgrade." | 20 min |
| **React components** | "Write a React component for a device status card. Shows device name, current temperature, online/offline dot, last updated. Use Tailwind." | 30 min |
| **API client code** | "Write a Python httpx client for the Grafana API. Methods: create_org, create_datasource, create_dashboard, create_alert_rule." | 1 hr |
| **Docker configs** | "Write a docker-compose.yml service definition for PostgreSQL 16 with healthcheck, persistent volume, and environment variables from .env." | 15 min |
| **Test writing** | "Write pytest tests for this FastAPI endpoint: [paste endpoint code]. Include happy path, 404, validation error, and auth required." | 30 min |
| **Bug investigation** | "Here's my error: [paste traceback]. Here's the relevant code: [paste]. What's wrong and how do I fix it?" | 15-60 min |
| **Code review** | "Review this code for bugs, security issues, and improvements: [paste]." | 15 min |
| **Documentation** | "Write API documentation for this endpoint: [paste]. Include request/response examples." | 20 min |
| **Config files** | "Write a Telegraf config that subscribes to MQTT topics matching +/from/+ and writes to InfluxDB with tags extracted from the topic." | 20 min |

#### Business & Admin (Save 1-2 hrs/week)

| Task | How to Delegate | Time Saved |
|------|----------------|:---:|
| **Meeting notes** | "Here are my rough notes from a client call: [paste]. Turn them into structured meeting notes with action items." | 15 min |
| **Invoice** | "Create an invoice for [company] for [services/products]. Include payment terms, bank details placeholder." | 15 min |
| **Contract draft** | "Draft a simple service agreement for IoT monitoring. Include: service description, pricing, data ownership, termination, liability limit." | 1 hr |
| **Pitch deck text** | "Write the text for a 10-slide pitch deck. Company: IoT monitoring for cold storage. Stage: pre-revenue, working prototype, 2 founders." | 1 hr |
| **FAQ document** | "Write a FAQ for potential clients. 15 questions covering: how it works, pricing, data security, installation, support, contract terms." | 30 min |

### 3.3 Things AI CANNOT Do (Must Be You)

```
DECISIONS:
  ✗ What to build next (AI can list options — you decide)
  ✗ Pricing decisions (AI can suggest — you set)
  ✗ Hiring/partnership decisions
  ✗ Architecture decisions (AI can propose — you evaluate)

RELATIONSHIPS:
  ✗ Client conversations (AI can draft emails — you send and respond)
  ✗ Partner communication (be human)
  ✗ Networking (AI can write the message — you click send and show up)
  ✗ Closing a sale (this requires trust, which requires a human)

JUDGMENT:
  ✗ "Is this prospect worth pursuing?"
  ✗ "Is this feature worth building?"
  ✗ "Is this code architecture right for our stage?"
  ✗ "Should I trust this person?"

QUALITY:
  ✗ Final review of anything AI writes (always review before sending)
  ✗ Testing that code actually works (AI generates, you verify)
  ✗ Making sure client-facing work matches your brand/voice
```

### 3.4 The AI Workflow in Your Day

```
MORNING HOUR (6:15-7:15):
  First 10 min:  Give AI 2-3 tasks for the day:
                 "Write a LinkedIn post about [topic]"
                 "Draft a follow-up email to [person]"
                 "Write the React component for [feature]"

  Next 50 min:   YOU do deep work (coding, thinking, designing)
                 while AI outputs sit ready for later review.

EVENING BLOCK (20:00-21:30):
  First 15 min:  Review AI outputs from morning.
                 Edit, approve, send, or iterate.

  Next 60 min:   Platform coding (with AI pair-programming as needed).
                 "Write tests for this function."
                 "How do I do [X] in TanStack Router?"
                 "Debug this error: [paste]"

  Last 15 min:   Queue up tomorrow's AI tasks:
                 "Research [prospect company]"
                 "Draft [document]"
                 "Write [code component]"

RESULT: AI does 2-3 hours of work for you while you sleep or work your day job.
```

## 4. How to Keep the Startup Moving During Baby Months

### 4.1 The Next 3 Months Plan (You're Solo on Platform)

Your partner will be at 2-5 hours/week. That's OK. Adjust the plan.

```
YOUR PRIORITIES (in order):
  1. Keep building the platform (you're the only one who can)
  2. Get to demo-ready (this is the milestone that matters)
  3. One LinkedIn post per week (AI writes draft, you edit)
  4. One prospect conversation per month (low volume, high quality)

PARTNER'S PRIORITIES (in order):
  1. Baby. Full stop.
  2. Keep one sensor prototype working (don't lose progress)
  3. Answer your questions async when possible
  4. Nothing else. Seriously.

WHAT PAUSES DURING BABY MONTHS:
  - On-site installations (need partner for hardware)
  - Active sales outreach (can't deliver pilots without hardware)
  - Events and networking (you don't have time either)
  - Any non-essential feature development

WHAT CONTINUES:
  - Platform development (you, steady pace)
  - AI-assisted content creation (LinkedIn, blog, materials)
  - Prospect research (AI does this, you review)
  - Advisor/mentor conversations (1 per month)
```

### 4.2 The "Baby-Proof" Milestone Plan

Adjust the sprint plan from TECH-LEAD-PLAYBOOK.md to reality:

```
MONTHS 1-3 (Baby arrives, partner at ~3 hrs/wk):
  Goal: Platform demo-ready on YOUR laptop/VPS
  You: Build login + dashboard + embedded charts + alerts
  Partner: Keep existing sensor working. Nothing new.
  AI: Drafts content, boilerplate code, research

MONTHS 3-5 (Partner recovering, ~6 hrs/wk):
  Goal: Partner builds 2-3 demo sensor units
  You: Polish platform, deploy to VPS, practice demo
  Partner: Hardware focus — build demo kit
  AI: Draft pitch materials, prospect research

MONTHS 5-7 (Partner at ~8 hrs/wk):
  Goal: First demo to a real prospect
  You: Run the demo, handle conversations
  Partner: On-site sensor installation for pilot
  Together: First pilot

MONTHS 7-9 (Both at ~12 hrs/wk):
  Goal: First paying client
  Normal operations resume.
```

**This is slower than the original 12-week plan. That's OK. You're building a business, not running a sprint. Sustainable pace wins.**

## 5. Protecting Family Time

### 5.1 Non-Negotiables

```
These are NOT optional. These are the foundation everything else rests on.

  ✓ Dinner with your kid every day
  ✓ Saturday afternoon: park / activity / whatever they want
  ✓ Sunday: family day (minimal startup, max 1 hour)
  ✓ Bedtime routine: yours, not your partner's backup
  ✓ One evening per week: partner time (no startup, no screens)
  ✓ Your kid's events (school play, birthday, sports): always attend
  ✓ Sleep 7 hours minimum (sacrificing sleep is borrowing from tomorrow)
```

### 5.2 What Your Kid Sees

```
BAD:  Dad is always on the laptop. Dad is stressed. Dad says "not now."
      Result: Your kid remembers you were absent during their childhood.

GOOD: Dad works early mornings before I wake up and some evenings
      after bedtime. On Saturday we go to the park.
      Dad is building something cool and sometimes shows me the
      blinking sensor light.
      Result: Your kid sees a parent who works hard AND shows up.
```

### 5.3 Involve the Kid (When It Makes Sense)

Your 5-year-old can't write code, but they can:
- Watch the blinking LED on the sensor and think it's magic
- "Help" you test by putting the sensor in the freezer and checking the temperature on your phone
- Draw a picture of what the "temperature robot" looks like
- Feel included instead of excluded

This isn't productivity. It's fatherhood. It matters more than the startup.

## 6. The Energy Management Framework

Time management is actually **energy management.** 2 hours with full energy > 5 hours exhausted.

### 6.1 When to Do What

| Energy Level | When | What to Do |
|:---:|------|-----------|
| **Peak** (sharp, creative) | Early morning (6:15-7:15) | Architecture decisions, complex coding, writing |
| **High** (focused, productive) | Saturday morning block | Feature building, demo prep, client work |
| **Medium** (functional, steady) | Weeknight evenings (20:00-21:30) | Routine coding, bug fixes, AI review |
| **Low** (tired, unfocused) | Late evenings, post-kid-bedtime | AI delegation (queue tasks), email, LinkedIn scrolling, light research |
| **Zero** (done) | Stop. | Sleep. Read a book. Watch something. Be human. |

### 6.2 Energy Drains to Avoid

| Drain | Why It Kills Your Energy | Alternative |
|-------|------------------------|-------------|
| Decision fatigue | 50 small decisions exhaust you more than 2 big ones | Batch decisions. Monday: decide what to build this week. Then just execute. |
| Context switching | Going from kid bedtime → code → email → LinkedIn → code → email | One task per block. Evening = coding. No email. |
| Perfectionism | Rewriting the same component 3 times | Ship it ugly. Fix it later. Done > perfect. |
| Comparison | "That other startup shipped in 3 months" | They don't have your job, your kid, or your life. Your timeline is yours. |
| Guilt | "I should be working" (during family time) or "I should be with my kid" (during startup time) | When you're with your kid, be WITH your kid. When you're coding, CODE. Fully present in both. |
| Social media | 30 minutes of scrolling = 30 minutes of coding lost | Airplane mode during startup blocks |

## 7. Tools for Organized Time

### 7.1 What You Need (Minimal)

| Tool | What For | Cost |
|------|---------|:---:|
| **Calendar** (Google Calendar) | Block startup time. If it's not on the calendar, it won't happen. | Free |
| **Task list** (Todoist, Notion, or a paper notebook) | Weekly priorities. Max 3 tasks per week. Not 30. | Free |
| **Timer** (phone timer or Pomodoro app) | 50-minute focused blocks. Stop when the timer stops. | Free |
| **Shared notes with partner** (Notion, Google Docs, or WhatsApp group) | Async communication. He can respond when the baby sleeps. | Free |
| **AI assistant** (Claude, ChatGPT) | Delegation machine. See Section 3. | Free-$20/mo |

### 7.2 The Weekly Planning Ritual (15 min, Sunday night)

```
Every Sunday at 21:00, spend 15 minutes:

1. REVIEW last week:
   What did I finish? What didn't happen? Why?

2. PICK 3 priorities for this week:
   Not 10. Not 7. THREE.
   "This week I will: [1], [2], [3]."
   Example:
     1. Build the login page
     2. Send 5 LinkedIn outreach messages (AI drafts them)
     3. Have a coffee meeting with [person]

3. BLOCK time on calendar:
   - Morning hours: which days am I waking early? (aim for 4-5)
   - Evening blocks: which 3 evenings? (tell your partner)
   - Saturday morning: what's the big task?

4. QUEUE AI tasks:
   List 3-5 things to delegate to AI this week.
   Morning Day 1: feed them in. Review outputs by evening.

5. CHECK partner alignment:
   Quick text: "This week I'm focusing on [X]. Anything you need from me?
   Anything you're working on?"
```

## 8. What Your Week Actually Looks Like

### A Realistic Good Week

```
MONDAY:
  6:15  Morning hour: code the device list API endpoint
  20:00 Evening: review AI-drafted LinkedIn post, edit, schedule.
        Continue coding device list frontend.
  21:30 Stop.

TUESDAY:
  6:15  Morning hour: code device detail page with iframe embed
  Evening: family/partner night. No startup.

WEDNESDAY:
  6:15  Morning hour: fix bugs from yesterday's code
  20:00 Evening: AI pair-programming session.
        "Write tests for my API endpoints."
        Review and adjust AI output. Push code.
  21:30 Stop.

THURSDAY:
  6:15  Morning hour: code alert configuration flow
  20:00 Evening: respond to LinkedIn messages.
        Research 3 prospect companies (AI helps).
        Draft outreach emails (AI writes, you personalize).
  21:30 Stop.

FRIDAY:
  6:15  Morning hour: code, code, code.
  Evening: family night. No startup.

SATURDAY:
  8:00-12:00  Big block: integrate alerts with Grafana API.
              Test end-to-end: sensor → dashboard → alert → email.
              Quick sync call with partner (20 min).
  12:00-18:00 Family time. Park. Playground. Ice cream.

SUNDAY:
  Rest. Maybe 30 min LinkedIn post prep.
  21:00: 15-minute planning for next week.

### A Realistic Bad Week

```
MONDAY:    Kid is sick. No morning hour. Too tired in evening.
TUESDAY:   Day job crisis. Work until 19:00. Exhausted.
WEDNESDAY: 6:15 morning hour: code for 50 min. Small progress.
THURSDAY:  Evening: 1 hour of coding. Not great but something.
FRIDAY:    Nothing. Ran out of steam.
SATURDAY:  8:00-10:00: 2 hours of coding. Kid has a birthday party at 10:30.
SUNDAY:    Rest.

TOTAL: ~4 hours. BAD WEEK.

BUT: This happens. 4 hours is not zero. One good week
erases one bad week. Don't guilt-spiral. Just start again Monday.
